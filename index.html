An intuitive web app for Japanese kanji exploration. Visualize how each kanji's frequency rank varies across contexts z for real-world usage insights. Quickly search and access detailed information like stroke order animations, component breakdowns, sample vocabulary and more.

Find kanji by English keyword, on'yomi reading, or kun'yomi reading via intuitive search bar. Then, sort and filter results by JLPT level, Jouyou Grade, Remembering the Kanji (RTK) index, Wanikani Level, stroke count, or frequency ranking and more. Clicking on any kanji to reveal sample vocabulary, component composition, stroke animations, how it looks like in various fonts and more.

Frequency Rank Data Sources
Netflix - Derived from Japanese Netflix subtitles containing about 53M total kanji occurrences. Compiled by OhTalkWho オタク (Dave Doebrick) in 2019.
Japanese Drama - Derived from 12,277 subtitles from Japanese drama, anime and films containing about 20.5M total kanji occurrences. Compiled by Chris Kempson. github.com/Matchoo95/JP-Subtitles chriskempson/japanese-subtitles-word-kanji-frequency-lists
Novel- Derived from scanning 5100 novels containing about 180M total kanji occurrences. Compiled by Redditor Nukemarine
Frequency Rankings By Dmitry Shpika scriptin.github.io/kanji-frequency
Twitter - Derived from Twitter messages collected last June 2015 containing about 10M total kanji occurrences.
Wikipedia = Derived from 100,000 Wikipedia articles sampled in 2023 and contained 59M total kanji occurrences.
Online News - Derived from over 3,700 articles from Japanese Wikinews that were published from 2005 to 2023 and contained 1.1M total kanji occurrences.
Aozora - Derived from over 17,000 books from Aozora Bunko, the vast majority of which are more than 70 years old, and contained 67.8M total kanji occurrences
Compiled by Patrick Kandrac.
Google - Kouji Shibano's Google Kanji Data from 2009 that processed 133B kanji occurrences.
Kanji Usage Frequency (KUF) from 2016 that processed 850M kanji occurrences from Wikipedia, Aozora, e-news, Twitter.
Matsushita's Character Database (MCD) from 2014 that processed 33M words from sources The Balanced Corpus of Contemporary Written Japanese (BCCWJ) and Yahoo知恵袋 aka Japanese Yahoo Answer.
Published by the Japanese Agency for Cultural Affairs (文化庁) on 2010 that processed 1.45M kanji from various sources including books, magazines, textbooks, newspapers such as Asahi and Yomiuri, websites, etc.
Jisho.org uses Alexandre Girardi's word frequency list published in 1998 that used 300,000 words found in 4 years of the Mainichi Newpaper.
Alex Yatskov's Wikipedia Kanji Frequency Report (WKFR) published in 2010 analyzed more than 500M kanji occurrences.
